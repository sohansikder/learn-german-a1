In German statements, the conjugated verb is ALWAYS in position 2.

Subject → VERB → Object → Time → Place
Type Example Translation
Statement Ich trinke Kaffee. I drink coffee.
Time first Morgens trinke ich Kaffee. In the mornings I drink coffee.
Yes/No Q Trinken Sie Kaffee? Do you drink coffee?
W-Question Was trinken Sie? What do you drink?
💡 V2 Rule: No matter what starts the sentence, the verb is always in the second position. If time comes first, the subject moves after the verb.

German Cultural Insights

Language is culture. Understanding these customs will help you communicate naturally.

🤝

Sie vs. Du — Formal & Informal

In German, "Sie" (capitalized) is the formal "you" — used with strangers, professors, and in professional settings. "Du" is informal — for friends, family, and peers. At university, students usually use "du" with each other but "Sie" with professors until invited otherwise. When in doubt, start with "Sie"!

🍞

Mahlzeit! — German Meal Culture

Germans take meals seriously. "Frühstück" (breakfast) is often bread with cheese and cold cuts. "Mittagessen" (lunch) is the main warm meal. "Abendessen" (dinner) is typically lighter — bread again! Saying "Guten Appetit!" before eating is expected. And never start eating before everyone is served.

♻️

Mülltrennung — Recycling

Germany is famous for its strict recycling system. You'll encounter Restmüll (residual waste), Biomüll (organic), Papier (paper), Gelber Sack (packaging), and Glas (glass — sorted by color!). The Pfand system gives you money back for returning bottles.

🏫

Uni-Leben — University Life

German university culture differs from many countries. The Mensa (cafeteria) serves affordable meals. Vorlesungen (lectures) are often large, while Seminare (seminars) are smaller and interactive. Students knock on the desk ("Klopfen") instead of clapping to show approval!

⏰

Pünktlichkeit — Punctuality

Germans value punctuality highly. Being "pünktlich" (on time) is essential. If you're meeting someone, arrive 5 minutes early. For university, "c.t." (cum tempore) means the class starts 15 minutes late, while "s.t." (sine tempore) means it starts exactly on time. Always check!

🏠

Ruhezeit — Quiet Hours

Germany has strict "Ruhezeiten" (quiet hours). Generally, you must keep noise low between 22:00 and 6:00, and all day on Sundays and public holidays. No drilling, loud music, or even vacuuming on Sundays! It's the law, and your neighbors will let you know if you forget.
